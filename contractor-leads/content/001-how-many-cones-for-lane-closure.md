# How Many Traffic Cones Do You Actually Need for a Lane Closure? (NJ MUTCD Guide)

For a typical single-lane closure on a 40 mph road in New Jersey, you need roughly **20–30 cones**: a taper of about 10 cones spaced 40 ft apart, plus cones through the buffer and work area at roughly 80 ft spacing. The exact count depends on your road's speed limit, lane width, and how long the closure runs. Here's how to work it out for your specific job — with real examples.

---

## The MUTCD Formula Everyone Uses

The federal *Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices* (MUTCD) gives you two formulas for taper length. **Taper length (L)** is the distance over which you transition traffic out of the lane — it's the "diagonal" section of cones before the lane is fully closed.

**For roads 40 mph and under:**
> L = (W × S²) ÷ 60

**For roads above 40 mph:**
> L = W × S

Where:
- **W** = lane width in feet (typically 10–12 ft on NJ roads)
- **S** = posted speed limit in mph

Once you have L, cone spacing in the taper is **L ÷ 10** (with a minimum of 10 cones). Through the **buffer zone** (the clear space between the taper and actual work) and **activity area** (where workers and equipment are), space cones at **2× the taper spacing**.

This is the same formula NJDOT inspectors use, the same one your TCP preparer will base the plan on, and the same math you'll hear if a job gets audited.

---

## Worked Example: Typical NJ 40 MPH Road Closure

Let's walk through the calculation for the most common scenario in Central NJ: a standard arterial road with a 12-foot lane and a 40 mph speed limit.

**Step 1 — Calculate taper length:**
L = (12 × 40²) ÷ 60 = (12 × 1,600) ÷ 60 = 19,200 ÷ 60 = **320 feet**

**Step 2 — Cone spacing in taper:**
320 ÷ 10 = 32 ft. The MUTCD has a practical minimum of 40 ft in many interpretations; round to 40 ft for safe compliance.

**Step 3 — Taper cone count:**
320 ft ÷ 40 ft = **8 cones** (round up to 10 — the MUTCD minimum is 10 cones in any taper).

**Step 4 — Buffer + activity area:**
Assume a 100-ft buffer and a 500-ft work zone (typical for a sewer lateral repair or utility cut). That's 600 ft total. Spacing = 80 ft.
600 ÷ 80 = **7.5 → 8 cones**

**Step 5 — Total + spares:**
10 (taper) + 8 (buffer/activity) = **18 minimum**. Add 25%: **23 cones to bring on site.**

That 23-cone number is your floor. It's what passes inspection. In practice, bring 25–28 to account for one cone being knocked over per vehicle impact and cones you may need to redirect pedestrians or block a driveway.

## Cone Count by Speed Limit — Quick Reference Table

The table below uses a standard **12-foot lane width** and a 500-foot work zone. Scale the work zone column up or down for longer or shorter jobs.

| Speed Limit | Taper Length (L) | Cone Spacing (taper) | Taper Cones | Buffer + 500-ft Work Zone Cones | **Total (no spares)** | **Bring (with 25% spare)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 25 mph | 125 ft | 15 ft | 10 | 7 | 17 | 22 |
| 35 mph | 245 ft | 25 ft | 10 | 6 | 16 | 20 |
| 40 mph | 320 ft | 40 ft | 10 | 8 | 18 | 23 |
| 45 mph | 540 ft | 54 ft | 10 | 5 | 15 | 19 |
| 55 mph | 660 ft | 66 ft | 10 | 5 | 15 | 19 |

A few things stand out here that trip people up:

- Higher-speed roads actually use **fewer cones through the work area** because the wider spacing is code-compliant. The taper is longer, but once you're past it, you have room to breathe.
- The 25 mph scenario uses more cones *per foot of taper* than the 55 mph scenario. Tight spacing in a low-speed urban zone is often more work than people expect.
- The MUTCD minimum of 10 taper cones applies even when the math gives you fewer — e.g., on a 45 mph road with a very short taper, you still need all 10.

## When You Need More Than the Minimum

The MUTCD numbers are a floor. Several common conditions require you to exceed them, and NJDOT inspectors know what to look for.

**Nighttime work.** NJDOT follows MUTCD Section 6F.64: all cones in a nighttime work zone on any road with a speed limit above 35 mph must be at least **36 inches tall with a retroreflective collar**. On roads 35 mph and under, 28-inch cones with double retroreflective banding are the minimum. 18-inch cones are daytime-only. If you're using the wrong cone at night and there's an incident, you're exposed.

**Curves and limited sight lines.** When the advance warning taper or the work zone itself goes around a bend, tighten the spacing. A common rule of thumb: if a driver rounding the curve has less than a 500-ft sight line to the first cone, halve your normal spacing.

**High-volume roads.** On roads with daily traffic counts above ~15,000 AADT (think Route 1, Route 9, many Middlesex and Monmouth County corridors), add one extra cone at the beginning and end of the taper as "doubling up" to give drivers redundant guidance. This is also a good idea when trucks make up a large share of traffic — they run wide.

**Adjacent pedestrian channels.** If your closure forces pedestrians to walk near the work zone, you need channelizing devices (typically cones or delineators) creating a continuous separation between foot traffic and equipment. The cone count for this runs separately from your vehicle-lane closure count.

**Prolonged closures.** For a closure lasting more than a few hours in place, cones that get knocked out of position should be reset frequently. Budget for 10–15% more cones than minimum to keep the setup full through the work day.

## What NJ Adds on Top of the Federal MUTCD

New Jersey adopts the federal MUTCD with state-specific amendments published by NJDOT. The key differences contractors run into most:

**Permit requirements vary by municipality.** A 40 mph road through Woodbridge Township may require more lead time and additional signing than the MUTCD minimum alone. Always check the municipal permit — it often specifies the exact TTC plan elements required for your road class. Middlesex County roads and NJDOT state routes each have their own plan approval processes.

**Overnight closures on state highways.** Any closure on an NJDOT-maintained road (Route 1, Route 9, Route 18, the GSP, Turnpike shoulders) requires pre-approval and often a Traffic Control Plan (TCP) stamped by a licensed engineer. NJDOT's lane-closure permitting system (NJ Permit Management, formerly e-NJTPA) includes specific cone count and type requirements per route.

**NJDOT Standard Specifications, Section 801.** This section governs temporary traffic control for all NJDOT-contracted work. If you're a sub on a DOT contract, your TCP must meet these specs, not just federal MUTCD.

For the current NJDOT Standard Specs and any state-specific amendments to the MUTCD, check [NJDOT's Standards & Specifications page](https://www.nj.gov/transportation/eng/specs/) directly — requirements can update mid-year.

## Cone Types Matter — Which Ones Pass Inspection?

Not all cones are equal. New Jersey's inspectors check size, reflectivity, and base weight.

**18-inch cones** — For parking lots, event management, and pedestrian guidance only. Not acceptable for lane closures on any road where vehicles travel.

**28-inch cones (7 lb base)** — The minimum for vehicle-speed work zones during daylight. These are the standard rental cone for NJ contractor jobs on roads up to 45 mph. Look for a single or double retroreflective collar. Single collar is the daytime minimum; double collar is required on most nighttime setups below 35 mph.

**36-inch cones (10 lb base)** — Required for nighttime work on roads over 35 mph and strongly recommended for any highway-speed closure (45 mph+) day or night. The heavier base keeps them standing when a car clips them at 55 mph; lightweight cones blow into traffic, creating secondary hazards.

For a fast check: if your cones don't have a prominent reflective collar visible 400+ feet away, they're not passing a nighttime inspection.

## The Math You Actually Use On-Site

When you're on the job with 15 minutes to set up and you don't have the MUTCD in your truck, here's the rule of thumb version:

**Quick formula by speed limit:**

| Speed Limit | Taper Cones | Spacing in Work Zone |
|---|---|---|
| 25–30 mph | 10 | 15 ft apart |
| 35–40 mph | 10 | 40 ft apart |
| 45–50 mph | 10 | 50–60 ft apart |
| 55+ mph | 10–12 | 80 ft apart |

"When in doubt, add 20%" is a real-world rule for a real reason: cones get moved by trucks, get placed crooked and slide, or get used to block a side-street driveway that nobody told you about. Starting with 20–25% more than your calculated minimum means the closure stays intact through the day without a re-supply run.

The cleanest on-site habit: **pace your spacing before you set cones**. Walk the taper once, counting paces, place a piece of tape or a chalk mark at each cone location. Then drop cones. Takes 3 minutes and eliminates the accordion effect where your first five cones are perfect and the last five are bunched up at the work area.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How far apart should traffic cones be in a work zone?**
In the taper, space cones at L ÷ 10 feet (where L is the calculated taper length). On a 40 mph road with a 12-ft lane, that's about 32 ft — round to 40 ft for clean compliance. Through the buffer and activity area, double the spacing: 80 ft on a 40 mph road.

**Do I need bigger cones at night?**
Yes. MUTCD requires 36-inch retroreflective cones for nighttime work on roads with a speed limit above 35 mph. 28-inch cones with double retroreflective collars are the minimum on lower-speed roads after dark. 18-inch cones are not acceptable for any vehicle-speed work zone at night.

**What's the minimum taper length on a 25 mph road?**
On a 25 mph road with a standard 12-ft lane: L = (12 × 625) ÷ 60 = 125 ft. Minimum 10 cones, spaced about 12.5 ft apart (round up to 15 ft). Always cross-check against your municipal permit, which sometimes exceeds the federal minimum.

**Can I mix 18-inch and 28-inch cones in the same work zone?**
The MUTCD doesn't explicitly prohibit it, but NJ inspectors expect uniformity. If the road is above 35 mph, 28-inch cones are the minimum for the entire setup. Mixing sizes is a red flag in an inspection.

**How many spare cones should I bring?**
Add 25% to your calculated minimum. On a job requiring 20 cones, bring 25. Cones get knocked over, repositioned, or borrowed mid-job. If you're renting, rounding up is typically cheap — and there's no cost to returning extras.
